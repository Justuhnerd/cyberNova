Malware

Malicious software — viruses, trojans, spyware, worms

High Risk

Malware (malicious software) is any program designed to harm, exploit, or gain unauthorised access to systems. Common entry vectors in Southern Africa:

  • →Phishing emails with malicious attachments or links — the single most common entry point across African SMEs
  • →Pirated software distributed via USB or local file-sharing networks, containing embedded keyloggers
  • →Drive-by downloads from compromised or fake websites that exploit unpatched browser vulnerabilities
  • →Infected USB drives — dominant in organisations with air-gapped or bandwidth-constrained networks
  • →Malvertising — malicious code embedded in online advertisements, even on legitimate news sites

African Context: Botswana's CERT recorded a 38% year-on-year spike in malware incidents in 2024, largely attributed to pirated business software in cash-constrained SMEs.

Virus

Self-replicating code that attaches to legitimate programs and spreads when files are shared or executed.

Trojan

Disguised as legitimate software — opens a backdoor once installed. Often delivered via fake downloads or email attachments.

Spyware

Silently monitors user activity, capturing passwords, banking credentials, browsing history, and financial data.

Rootkit

Hides deep in the OS, giving attackers persistent, undetected administrative access that survives reboots.

Worm

Spreads automatically across networks without user interaction — devastating in flat, unsegmented architectures.

Adware

Displays unwanted ads and harvests browsing data. Often a precursor to browser hijacking.

Fileless Malware

Operates entirely in memory using legitimate OS tools. Leaves no file on disk — evades most traditional antivirus.

Cryptojacker

Silently hijacks your CPU to mine cryptocurrency for attackers. Degrades system performance and inflates energy costs.

CyberNova deploys a layered defence combining EDR tooling, network traffic analysis, and behavioural AI that detects malware even without known signatures. Our Adaptive Threat Intel team maintains curated IOC feeds covering active campaigns in the SADC region.

Beyond signature scanning, we monitor process behaviour, memory anomalies, and lateral movement patterns — catching even living-off-the-land techniques that conventional tools miss.

Ransomware

File encryption attacks demanding cryptocurrency payment

Critical

Ransomware encrypts your files and demands payment — typically in cryptocurrency — to restore access. A modern attack follows this chain:

1

Initial Access — via phishing, exposed RDP, or credentials bought from a dark web broker

2

Persistence & Recon — plants backdoors, silently maps the network, identifies backup systems and domain controllers

3

Lateral Movement — escalates privileges using tools like Mimikatz, moves across the network to reach critical systems

4

Data Exfiltration — sensitive data copied out before encryption (enabling double-extortion threats)

5

Detonation — all accessible files encrypted simultaneously, backups deleted, ransom note deployed on every screen

African Context: LockBit, BlackCat, and Cl0p affiliates actively target Southern African organisations. Average ransom demand: USD $280,000. Average dwell time before detonation: 18-21 days.

RaaS is a criminal business model where ransomware developers lease their attack tools to affiliates in exchange for a cut of ransom payments — typically 20-30%. This mirrors legitimate SaaS platforms: there are dashboards, customer support portals, and even negotiation services for victims who want to pay.

The result: ransomware is now commoditised. Affiliates need no technical expertise — just the ability to phish credentials or buy access. Active RaaS platforms targeting SADC include LockBit 3.0, BlackCat/ALPHV, Play, and Akira.

No — and here is why: Only 65% of organisations that pay actually recover all their data. Paying marks you as a "paying target" and doubles your likelihood of a repeat attack within 12 months. In some jurisdictions, paying sanctioned groups carries legal risk.

CyberNova's incident response team has recovered multiple client environments without ransom payment through offline backup restoration, forensic decryption, and shadow copy recovery. Prevention and resilient backups are always the correct strategy.

The most effective ransomware defence is resilience — making recovery fast and cheap regardless of what attackers do. Key controls:

  • →3-2-1 backup rule — with at least one immutable, air-gapped copy
  • →Patch everything — 60% of ransomware entries exploit known, patchable vulnerabilities on internet-facing systems
  • →Disable exposed RDP or place behind MFA and a VPN gateway
  • →Segment your network — attackers cannot encrypt what they cannot reach; flat networks are ransomware's best friend
  • →Test backups quarterly — untested backups frequently fail when they are needed most

Phishing

Deceptive messages, smishing, vishing, and BEC attacks

Very Common
Email Phishing

Mass deceptive emails impersonating trusted brands. Load-shedding notifications and SASSA payment updates are widely exploited across South Africa.

Smishing

SMS attacks exploiting mobile banking usage across SADC, impersonating Standard Bank, FNB, Econet, or Airtel Money with urgency: "Your account is locked."

Vishing

Phone calls from fraudsters impersonating bank fraud departments or IT helpdesks, manipulating staff into revealing credentials or OTPs in real time.

Spear Phishing

Highly targeted email using harvested personal details — LinkedIn job titles, recent contract names, colleague relationships — to appear completely legitimate.

BEC / Whaling

Impersonating the CEO or CFO to approve fraudulent payments. Often involves no malware at all — purely social manipulation targeting finance teams.

No single indicator is definitive, but these red flags — especially in combination — signal a phishing attempt:

Urgency or threats

"Your account closes in 24 hours" — attackers force fast decisions that bypass critical thinking.

Mismatched sender domain

From "support@firstnat-ional-bank.co.za" — always hover sender addresses to reveal the real domain.

Generic salutation

"Dear Customer" instead of your name — bulk phishing campaigns cannot personalise at scale.

Unexpected attachments

.zip, .exe, .docm, or password-protected files — designed to bypass email security filters.

Suspicious links

Hover before clicking — displayed text and actual URL rarely match in phishing attacks.

Unusual payment requests

A CFO asking for an urgent wire transfer via email only, bypassing normal dual-approval processes.

Technology alone cannot stop phishing — it always exploits the human layer. But these controls reduce exposure significantly:

  • →MFA everywhere — even if credentials are phished, MFA prevents account takeover in 99.9% of cases
  • →DMARC, DKIM, and SPF email authentication — prevents your domain being spoofed to attack your own clients
  • →Email filtering with sandboxing — detonates suspicious attachments in an isolated environment before delivery
  • →Phishing simulation training — regular, unannounced simulations that teach staff to recognise attacks in their specific context

DDoS Attacks

Distributed Denial of Service — flooding services to cause outages

Disruptive

A DDoS attack uses a botnet to flood a target until its services become unavailable. Modern attacks reach terabit-scale within minutes.

Volumetric

Saturates bandwidth with junk traffic. Measured in Gbps/Tbps. Overwhelms internet connections upstream.

Protocol

Exploits network protocol weaknesses. SYN floods exhaust server resources without saturating bandwidth.

Application Layer

Targets web application logic with requests that look legitimate but consume disproportionate server resources.

Regional Impact: South African banking portals, Zimbabwe government services, and Zambian telecoms have all experienced significant DDoS campaigns since 2022. Anonymous Sudan targeted multiple African financial institutions in 2024.

DDoS mitigation works by absorbing, filtering, or dispersing attack traffic before it reaches your infrastructure:

  • →Scrubbing centres — CyberNova routes your traffic through scrubbing infrastructure that strips attack packets in real time
  • →Anycast routing — distributes attack traffic across multiple global points of presence
  • →Rate limiting and WAF — blocks application-layer attacks before they consume server resources
  • →ISP null-routing — pre-agreed escalation procedures to block attack sources upstream at the ISP level

RDDoS (Ransom Denial of Service) is an extortion tactic where criminals demand cryptocurrency to prevent a DDoS attack. They often launch a small demonstration attack first to prove capability. Do not pay — payment does not guarantee the attack stops and marks you for future extortion. Contact CyberNova immediately to activate DDoS protection before any threatened deadline.

Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs)

Nation-state and organised long-term intrusion campaigns

Sophisticated

APTs are distinguished by three characteristics: Advanced (using custom tooling and zero-days), Persistent (maintaining access for months or years undetected), and Targeted (focused on specific organisations or infrastructure).

Average dwell time in African government networks: 287 days. CyberNova tracks 6 APT groups known to operate in the SADC region.

African Context: State-attributed APT groups have been observed targeting SADC ministries, central banks, and energy regulators since 2020.

A supply chain attack targets a trusted vendor or software provider to gain access to all their customers at once. The attacker inserts malicious code into a legitimate software update — every customer who installs the update is compromised. The SolarWinds and 3CX attacks used this technique to breach thousands of organisations including government agencies globally.

Defence requires SBOM tracking, third-party risk assessments, and monitoring that detects unusual behaviour from normally trusted software.

APTs evade standard detection. Finding them requires threat hunting — actively searching for anomalies:

  • →Admin accounts active at 3am in a timezone that does not match your offices
  • →Slow, low-volume data egress to unfamiliar external IPs — APTs move data slowly to stay below alert thresholds
  • →Lateral movement — service accounts accessing systems they have never touched before
  • →DNS queries to newly registered domains or randomised subdomains indicating DNS tunnelling
emergency

Zero-Day Exploits

Attacks targeting unpatched, undisclosed software vulnerabilities

High Severity

A zero-day is a flaw unknown to the software vendor — no patch exists. Attackers exploit them with near-100% success rates until a fix is released. Zero-days are traded on dark web markets for hundreds of thousands to millions of dollars. Nation-state intelligence agencies are the primary buyers.

Once a zero-day is used publicly, researchers typically discover it within days — triggering an emergency patch race. Until deployed, every organisation running the vulnerable software remains fully exposed.

You cannot patch the unknown — but you can limit blast radius and detect exploitation through behaviour-based controls:

  • →Behavioural EDR — detects the behaviour of exploitation (memory injection, process spawning anomalies) even without a known signature
  • →Virtual patching — blocks known exploit patterns at the network perimeter while waiting for the official fix
  • →Attack surface reduction — disable features you do not use; every enabled service is a potential zero-day target
  • →Network segmentation — limits blast radius so a zero-day in one system cannot cascade across the entire network
  • →CyberNova threat intelligence — emergency advisory within hours of any significant zero-day public disclosure

IoT Vulnerabilities

Internet of Things — smart devices as network entry points

Growing Risk

IoT devices typically ship with hardcoded default credentials, receive infrequent firmware updates, and are forgotten on networks for years. In Southern Africa, rapid smart infrastructure deployment has dramatically outpaced security hardening.

Compromised IoT devices serve as persistent network footholds or are weaponised into botnets. The Mirai botnet, responsible for some of the largest DDoS attacks in history, was built entirely from compromised cameras and routers with default passwords still in place.

  • →Change all default credentials immediately — "admin/admin" and "admin/password" are the first combinations attackers try
  • →Segment IoT onto a dedicated VLAN — isolate them so a compromised camera cannot reach your finance servers
  • →Maintain a complete device inventory — you cannot protect what you do not know is on your network
  • →Patch firmware quarterly — set calendar reminders to check manufacturer sites for updates
  • →Disable unnecessary services — if a camera does not need remote internet access, block it at the firewall

OT (Operational Technology) controls physical industrial processes. Unlike IT, OT systems were designed for availability and safety — not security. An OT breach does not just cause data loss; it can shut down power stations, contaminate water supplies, or halt manufacturing.

Given Southern Africa's energy infrastructure challenges, CyberNova's OT security practice specifically addresses SCADA and ICS environments across the SADC region, where attacks on critical infrastructure are increasingly frequent.

Social Engineering

Psychological manipulation of staff to bypass technical controls

Human Risk

Social engineering exploits human psychology rather than software. Attackers leverage six universal principles: authority (impersonating a CEO), urgency ("act now or lose access"), social proof ("your colleague already approved this"), scarcity, liking, and reciprocity.

Technology cannot reliably distinguish a legitimate executive instruction from a fraudulent one — only a trained human can. BEC scams are the single most financially damaging attack category in Africa. The only effective defence is a trained human layer combined with out-of-band verification procedures for high-value transactions.

Pretexting involves constructing a believable false identity and scenario. Common pretexts used against African organisations:

  • →IT support impersonation — calling helpdesk claiming to be a new staff member locked out, requesting a password reset without verification
  • →Auditor or regulator — claiming to be from SARB, NBFIRA, or a tax authority demanding immediate access to financial systems
  • →Vendor invoice fraud — impersonating a trusted supplier and requesting banking details be updated to a fraudulent account
  • →New employee onboarding — impersonating HR to collect employee data or redirect direct deposit banking information
  • →Callback verification — always call back using a number from official records, never the number provided in the suspicious contact
  • →Dual-approval for payments — no single person can authorise a wire transfer above a threshold without a second approver via a separate channel
  • →Safe reporting culture — staff must feel psychologically safe to question unusual requests without fear of reprimand, even from executives
  • →Regular simulation exercises — CyberNova's Security Awareness Training includes unannounced physical and digital social engineering tests with personalised debrief

Insider Threats

Malicious or negligent employees, contractors, and partners

Often Overlooked

Malicious Insider

Intentionally steals data, sabotages systems, or facilitates external attackers. Most dangerous because they have legitimate access and know your systems.

Negligent Insider

Causes breaches through carelessness — clicking phishing links, weak passwords, misconfigurations. Accounts for approximately 60% of insider incidents.

Compromised Insider

A legitimate employee whose credentials or device have been taken over by an external attacker — creating an insider threat without any employee wrongdoing.

Detection requires UEBA tooling that flags deviations: accessing data outside normal hours, bulk downloads before resignation, or accessing systems outside job role scope.

PAM limits what damage any single insider can cause by enforcing least-privilege access, vaulting privileged credentials, recording all privileged sessions, and requiring just-in-time access approvals for sensitive systems. PAM also creates an immutable audit trail for forensic investigation.

Critical offboarding: Immediately revoke all access (not just primary accounts), retrieve devices, cycle shared credentials, and audit recent activity on departure. Most insider data theft occurs in the two weeks before resignation.

Data Exfiltration

Unauthorised transfer of sensitive data to external parties

POPIA Relevant

POPIA requires notification to the Information Regulator and affected data subjects as soon as reasonably possible after discovering a data breach. Non-compliance risks fines up to R10 million or 10 years imprisonment for the Information Officer.

Botswana's Data Protection Act similarly mandates breach reporting. Zimbabwe's CDPA 2021 is now enforced. CyberNova maintains a breach response retainer that activates within the hour — including regulatory notification drafting and evidence preservation.

Sophisticated attackers use covert channels designed to blend with normal traffic:

DNS tunnelling

Encodes stolen data inside DNS queries — a protocol most firewalls allow unconditionally. Slow but nearly invisible to standard monitoring.

HTTPS to cloud services

Uploading data to OneDrive, Google Drive, or Dropbox — traffic looks identical to legitimate cloud sync activity.

Low-and-slow exfil

Moving small amounts of data over weeks or months — staying below alert thresholds triggered by large bulk transfers.

Steganography

Hiding stolen data inside innocent-looking image or audio files uploaded to public platforms — bypasses content inspection entirely.

Detection requires DLP tools with behavioural baselining, encrypted traffic inspection, and anomalous DNS query monitoring — all components of CyberNova's Sentinel platform.

You cannot protect data you have not identified. A data classification framework is foundational:

RESTRICTED

PII, financial records, health data, board communications — encrypted at rest and in transit, access logged, strictly need-to-know only

CONFIDENTIAL

Internal strategy documents, contracts, HR data — encrypted, role-based access, not shareable externally without approval

INTERNAL

General internal communications and operational data — accessible to all staff but not for external distribution

PUBLIC

Marketing materials, published reports — no restrictions on distribution
