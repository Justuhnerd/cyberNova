Malware

Malicious software — viruses, trojans, spyware, worms

High Risk

Malware (malicious software) is any program designed to harm, exploit, or gain unauthorised access to systems. It enters your environment through several vectors common in Southern Africa:

  • →Phishing emails with malicious attachments or links — the most common entry point across African SMEs
  • →Pirated software distributed via USB or local file-sharing networks, frequently containing embedded keyloggers
  • →Drive-by downloads from compromised or fake websites
  • →Infected USB drives — a major vector in organisations with limited network access

African Context: South Africa, Zimbabwe, and Botswana see high rates of malware from pirated business software, particularly in SMEs that cannot afford licensed tools.

Virus

Self-replicating code that attaches to legitimate programs and spreads when files are shared.

Trojan

Disguised as legitimate software — opens a backdoor for attackers once installed.

Spyware

Silently monitors user activity, capturing passwords, browsing, and financial data.

Rootkit

Hides deep in the OS, giving attackers persistent, undetected administrative access.

Worm

Spreads automatically across networks without user interaction — devastating in flat network architectures.

Adware

Displays unwanted ads and may harvest browsing data. Often a precursor to more serious infections.

Ransomware

File encryption attacks demanding cryptocurrency payment

Critical

Ransomware encrypts your files and demands payment — typically in cryptocurrency — to restore access. A typical attack follows this sequence:

1

Initial Access — via phishing email, exposed RDP, or compromised credentials

2

Lateral Movement — attacker moves through the network, escalating privileges and identifying backup systems

3

Exfiltration — data copied out before encryption (enabling double-extortion threats)

4

Encryption — all accessible files encrypted, ransom note deployed

African Context: LockBit, BlackCat, and cl0p affiliates actively target Southern African logistics, healthcare, and financial institutions. Average ransom demand in the region: USD $280,000.

RaaS is a criminal business model where ransomware developers lease their attack tools and infrastructure to affiliates in exchange for a cut of ransom payments (typically 20–30%). This is why ransomware attacks have exploded — the technical barrier is now near zero. Affiliates only need to identify victims and gain initial access.

It mirrors legitimate SaaS: there are dashboards, customer support, negotiation portals, and even "satisfaction guarantees" to victims who pay.

No — and here's why: Only 65% of organisations that pay actually recover all their data. Paying marks you as a "paying target" and increases likelihood of re-attack. It funds criminal operations and in some jurisdictions may have legal implications depending on which group is sanctioned.

Phishing

Deceptive messages, smishing, vishing, and BEC attacks

Very Common
Email Phishing

Mass deceptive emails impersonating trusted brands — banks, SARS, telecoms providers. Load-shedding notifications are widely exploited in South Africa.

Smishing

SMS attacks exploiting mobile banking usage rates across SADC, often impersonating Standard Bank, FNB, or Econet.

Spear Phishing

Highly targeted email using personal details — LinkedIn-sourced job titles, recent contracts — to appear legitimate.

BEC / Whaling

Impersonating the CEO or CFO to approve fraudulent transfers. The most costly single attack type in African finance — average loss: USD $130,000 per incident.

DDoS Attacks

Distributed Denial of Service — flooding services to cause outages

Disruptive

A DDoS attack uses a botnet of thousands of hijacked devices to flood a target with traffic until its services become unavailable. Even well-resourced infrastructure can buckle under terabit-scale attacks.

Typical attackers targeting Southern Africa: hacktivist groups protesting government policies, criminal organisations offering "DDoS-for-hire" services, and competitors in competitive industries.

Regional Impact: South African banking and e-commerce portals, Zimbabwean government services, and Zambian telecoms have all experienced significant DDoS campaigns since 2022.

radar

Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs)

Nation-state and organised long-term intrusion campaigns

Sophisticated

APTs are distinguished by three characteristics: they are Advanced (using custom tooling and zero-days), Persistent (maintaining access for months or years without detection), and Targeted (focused on specific organisations, ministries, or infrastructure).

Average dwell time for an APT in African government networks: 287 days — meaning attackers remain undetected for nearly 10 months. During this time they harvest classified documents, credentials, procurement data, and diplomatic communications.

African Context: APT groups attributed to foreign states have been observed targeting SADC ministries, central banks, and energy regulators since 2020. CyberNova's threat intelligence team actively tracks 6 APT groups known to operate in the region.

emergency

Zero-Day Exploits

Attacks targeting unpatched, undisclosed software vulnerabilities

High Severity

A zero-day is a security flaw that is unknown to the software vendor, meaning there is no available patch. Attackers who discover — or purchase — zero-days can exploit them with near-100% success rates until the vendor issues a fix.

Zero-days are traded on dark web markets for hundreds of thousands of dollars. They are primarily used by nation-state actors and sophisticated criminal groups.

IoT Vulnerabilities

Internet of Things — smart devices as network entry points

Growing Risk

IoT devices often ship with weak default credentials, receive infrequent firmware updates, and lack built-in security controls. In Southern Africa, rapid smart infrastructure deployment has outpaced security hardening. Change all default passwords immediately.

Social Engineering

Psychological manipulation of staff to bypass technical controls

Human Risk

Social engineering exploits human psychology rather than software vulnerabilities. BEC scams targeting finance teams are the most financially damaging social engineering attack in Africa. The only effective defence is a well-trained human layer — which is why CyberNova's Security Awareness Training is critical.

Insider Threats

Malicious or negligent employees, contractors, and partners

Often Overlooked

Malicious, negligent, and compromised insiders. Detection requires UEBA tooling and least-privilege access controls — components of CyberNova's framework.

Data Exfiltration

POPIA Relevant

POPIA requires notification to the Information Regulator and affected data subjects. Non-compliance can result in fines up to R10 million or imprisonment.
